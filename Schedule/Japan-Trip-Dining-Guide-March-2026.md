#### 🍜 Lunch Options (Asakusa Area - Near Senso-ji Temple)

**Fine Dining & Traditional:**

1. **Asakusa Imahan** (浅草今半)
   - **Specialty**: Premium Sukiyaki (120+ years history)
   - **Price**: ¥5,000-15,000
   - **Why Go**: One of Japan's most famous sukiyaki restaurants, high-quality wagyu beef with secret sauce formula
   - **Location**: 5-min walk from Senso-ji Temple

2. **Kaminarimon Sansada** (雷門三定)
   - **Specialty**: Traditional Tempura
   - **Price**: ¥2,000-4,000
   - **Why Go**: Authentic tempura in traditional teahouse, using traditional sesame oil blend
   - **Perfect For**: Traditional Japanese experience

3. **Sugita** (すぎ田)
   - **Specialty**: Tonkatsu (Breaded Deep-Fried Pork Cutlet)
   - **Price**: ¥1,500-3,000
   - **Recognition**: Michelin Bib Gourmand for 4 consecutive years
   - **Why Go**: Best tonkatsu in Asakusa

**Noodles & Casual:**

4. **Owariya** (尾張屋)
   - **Specialty**: Soba, Tempura, Udon
   - **Price**: ¥1,000-2,500
   - **Why Go**: Long-established restaurant, incredibly famous among locals
   - **Location**: Between Asakusa and Tawara Stations

5. **Onigiri Asakusa Shukuroku** (おにぎり浅草宿六)
   - **Specialty**: Onigiri (Rice Balls)
   - **Price**: ¥300-800
   - **Why Go**: Tokyo's oldest onigiri shop (since 1954), Koshihikari rice cooked in traditional iron pot
   - **Perfect For**: Quick, authentic snack

**Eel Specialty:**

6. **Koyanagi** (小柳)
   - **Specialty**: Unagi (Eel)
   - **Price**: ¥3,000-6,000
   - **Why Go**: Featured on many TV programs, original sauce, supported by regulars
   - **Perfect For**: Traditional eel experience

#### 🍖 Dinner Options (Shinjuku - Near Hotel Groove)

**Hotel Area (Kabukicho):**

3. **JAM17 Dining** (In Tokyu Kabukicho Tower - same building as your hotel!)

4. **Junisoh** (純和荘) at Hilton Tokyo

## 📝 Dining Tips for Japan

### Reservation Tips
- **Language**: Many restaurants accept reservations via phone (Japanese), hotel concierge, or online platforms
- **Timing**: Book 2-3 days ahead for popular spots, especially Asakusa Imahan and Manchinro Honten
- **Walk-ins**: Most casual spots (ramen, gyoza, onigiri) don't need reservations

### Payment
- **Cash Preferred**: Many traditional restaurants still prefer cash
- **Credit Cards**: Upscale restaurants and hotels accept cards
- **IC Cards**: Suica/Pasmo work at some chain restaurants
- **Bring**: ¥20,000-30,000 cash for food over 3 days

### Dining Etiquette
- **Remove Shoes**: Some traditional restaurants require shoe removal
- **Oshibori**: Use hot towel before eating, not during
- **Chopsticks**: Never stick upright in rice, don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick
- **Slurping**: Acceptable and encouraged for noodles!
- **Tipping**: NOT customary in Japan - considered rude

### Useful Phrases
- **Sumimasen** (すみません) = Excuse me / Thank you
- **Kore kudasai** (これください) = This one, please (point at menu)
- **Oishii** (おいしい) = Delicious!
- **Gochisousama deshita** (ごちそうさまでした) = Thank you for the meal (after eating)
- **Okaikei onegaishimasu** (お会計お願いします) = Check, please

### Dietary Restrictions
- **Vegetarian**: Challenging in traditional restaurants (dashi contains fish)
- **Halal**: Limited options, check Halal Gourmet Japan website
- **Allergies**: Learn Japanese words:
  - Ebi (shrimp), Kani (crab), Tamago (egg), Gyuniku (beef), Butaniku (pork)

## 📸 Food Photography Etiquette

### When Taking Photos
- **Upscale Restaurants**: Ask permission first ("Shashin wa daijoubu desu ka?")
- **Casual Places**: Generally OK, but be discreet
- **Markets**: OK for food displays, ask before photographing vendors
- **Avoid**: Flash photography in dim restaurants
- **Respectful**: Take photos before eating, not during
